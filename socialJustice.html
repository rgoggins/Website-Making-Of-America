Intro

Lowell quickly became a hotspot of new ideas, creating activists for many different social justice movements. Many different political petitions began in Lowell, including ones demanding a ten-hour work day, the abolition of slavery in Washington, DC, and the annexation of Texas.

Women's rights

The women of Lowell were often the fiercest fighters for justice. Despite not being able to vote, they demonstrated their political power by forming organizations such as Lowell Female Labor Reform Association and the Lowell Female Anti-Slavery Society. The table to the left was published in The Liberator, a Boston-located abolitionist newspaper. The table demonstrated the different petitions in the Massachusetts area. It was separated between males and females, and it is important to note that the petition with the most signatures belonged to females in Lowell.

Abolitionism

Lowell, a town that owed a lot of its economic strength to the horrors of the institution of slavery, surprisingly became a major force in the fight for abolitionism. Lowell, as well as other major Northern textile manufacturing towns such as Lawrence, Manchester, NH, was aware of the hundreds of thousands of slaves that toiled in the fields to provide their industry. However, “profits came before conscience” to many of the mill owners, seeing for a while in these cities, abolitionist movements springing up in the area only after the economy of these industrial centers was strong and stable.
